How football works

How long is a game, actually?

A game is split into four quarters of 15 minutes each, with a longer break at halftime (after quarter 2). But the clock stops constantly — incomplete passes, players going out of bounds, penalties — so a game actually takes about 3 hours to finish in real time, not one hour.

Four quarters of 15 minutes, with a break at halftime — like two rugby halves split into four. The catch: the clock stops constantly for incomplete passes, players stepping out, and penalties, so a game runs about 3 hours in real time, much longer than an 80-minute rugby match.

Four 15-minute quarters, halftime after quarter 2. Real-world runtime is closer to 3 hours because the clock stops often — commercial breaks and replay reviews stretch it further.

Standard four-quarter, 15-minute structure with a 12-minute halftime. Regular season ties go to a single 10-minute overtime; if still tied, it ends in a draw. Playoffs use full overtime periods until there's a winner.

Who does what — offense

The team with the ball is on offense, trying to move it forward and score.

The team with the ball is on offense — same idea as your team being in possession, driving forward phase by phase.

Quarterback (QB)

Gets the ball first, throws passes or hands it off. Closest thing to a flyhalf.

Running Back (RB)

Carries the ball on the ground. Think inside centre — takes it up the middle.

Wide Receiver (WR)

Runs downfield to catch passes. Similar to a winger chasing space.

Tight End (TE)

Catches passes and blocks — a hybrid role. Part loose forward, part receiver.

Offensive Line

Five players who protect the QB and clear running lanes. Your front row and locks, essentially.

Who does what — defense

The other team is on defense, trying to stop the offense and take the ball back.

Defensive Line

Rushes the QB and stops runs at the line. Front-row equivalent, going forward.

Linebacker (LB)

Stops both runs and short passes — the defense's all-rounders. Like your loose forwards.

Cornerback (CB)

Covers receivers one-on-one to stop passes. Marks a winger, essentially.

Safety

Plays deepest, covers the biggest plays. Similar role to a fullback.

Special teams — the third unit

There's also a special teams unit for kicks — field goals, punts, and kickoffs. It only comes on for those specific plays, not the whole game.

Special teams covers kickoffs, punts, and field goal attempts — a separate roster of specialists (kicker, punter, long snapper, returners) who only take the field for those situational plays.
